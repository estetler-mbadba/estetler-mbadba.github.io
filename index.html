The certification stack — sequencing matters

Three rules for stacking certifications around an online BBA:

  1. Free / cheap first. Bloomberg Market Concepts and Excel cost almost nothing and you can show them on LinkedIn this summer. They unlock interviews; nothing else on this list does that as fast.
  2. License before designation. SIE → Series 7 → Series 66 is the regulator path; CFA / CFP are the designation path. Do a license-track exam (SIE) before junior-year internship interviews — it tells employers you're sponsor-ready.
  3. Big credentials match the work, not the calendar. Don't start CFA until you're sure of buy-side / research interest. Don't start CFP until you've felt out wealth management. Both are 300+ hour commitments.

The local-network playbook (online degree edition)

An online BBA gives you something a traditional student does not have: time and geographic freedom. Spend it building local social capital. Three rails, in priority order:

  1. Toastmasters — pick a club that meets in-person, weekly, near you. Stay for 18+ months. Speaking reps + a tight cohort of professionals (often 10–25 years your senior) who will become references. Aim for Competent Communicator by the end of junior year and an officer role in senior year. This is the single highest-leverage activity for an online student.
  2. Chamber of Commerce — three layers: Young Professionals (your peer network), committees like Economic Development or Finance (where you sit across the table from local CFOs and bank presidents), and Business After Hours (low-pressure recurring exposure). Don't just attend — volunteer to take meeting notes once. You will be remembered.
  3. Targeted financial-industry orgs: CFA Society local chapter (student membership, often $40/yr — they actively recruit students), FPA (Financial Planning Association) local chapter, AAII (American Association of Individual Investors) study group, local SCORE / SBDC volunteer opportunities. Pick one, not all three.

One more thing. The university CAPS program almost certainly has a career-services office that 80% of online students never use. Schedule a 30-minute intro call this term. They have employer relationships you don't — and you're already paying for it.
